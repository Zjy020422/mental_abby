Knowledge about mental disorders

Gain a thorough understanding of the symptoms, causes and treatment methods of common mental disorders. A correct understanding of mental health issues is the first step to achieving effective treatment.

Bipolar Disorder

Global prevalence rate: 2.4% - 4.7%

Brief introduction

Bipolar disorder is a chronic mental illness characterized by extreme fluctuations in mood, energy and activity levels. The patient will experience alternating episodes of mania or hypomanic episodes with depressive episodes. This disease usually begins in adolescence or early adulthood and affects approximately 2.4% to 4.7% of the global population.
Bipolar disorder is classified into several types: Type I bipolar disorder (with at least one manic episode), Type II bipolar disorder (with at least one hypomanic episode and one major depressive episode), and cyclothymic disorder (with hypomanic and mild depressive symptoms lasting for at least two years). If left untreated, this disease may lead to severe functional impairment and a decline in quality of life.

Cardinal symptoms

Symptoms of a manic episode

  • Abnormally elevated mood or irritability
  • Excessive inflation of self-esteem
  • Significantly decreased demand for sleep
  • Being talkative and speaking fast
  • Wandering thoughts and scattered attention
  • Increased goal-oriented activities
  • Participating in high-risk activities

Symptoms of a depressive episode

  • Persistent low mood
  • Loss of interest or pleasure
  • Significant changes in body weight
  • Insomnia or excessive sleepiness
  • Psychomotor agitation or retardation
  • Fatigue or lack of energy
  • Feeling of worthlessness or excessive guilt
  • Reduced thinking or attention
  • Keep thinking of death

Epidemiological data

Prevalence statistics

• Global lifetime prevalence: For type I bipolar disorder, it is approximately 0.6% to 1.0%, and for type II, it is about 0.4% to 1.1%
• Prevalence rate in China: Approximately 0.4% to 1.0%, with an estimated 4 to 10 million patients
• Age of onset: The first onset usually occurs between the ages of 15 and 25
• Gender difference: The prevalence rates of men and women are basically equal
• Suicide risk: The suicide rate among patients with bipolar disorder is 15 to 20 times higher than that of the general population

Treatment and Management

The treatment of bipolar disorder usually involves a combination of medication and psychotherapy. Mood stabilizers (such as lithium salts, valproic acid, and lamotrigine) are at the core of treatment and can prevent the recurrence of manic and depressive episodes. Antipsychotic drugs and antidepressants may also be used depending on specific circumstances.
In terms of psychotherapy, cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT), interpersonal and social rhythm therapy (IPSRT), and family-focused therapy have all been proven effective. Patient education and family support are equally important, helping to identify early warning signs, maintain treatment compliance and establish a healthy lifestyle.

Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD)

Prevalence rate of children: 5.9% - 7.1%

Brief introduction

ADHD is a neurodevelopmental disorder characterized by persistent inattention, hyperactivity and impulsive behavior patterns, which can interfere with function and development. ADHD usually begins to manifest in childhood, but many people are not diagnosed until adulthood.
ADHD is divided into three types of manifestations: inattentive type, hyperactive-impulsive type and mixed type. Symptoms must occur in two or more Settings (such as home and school) and significantly affect social, academic or occupational functions. Although ADHD cannot be cured, its symptoms can be effectively managed through appropriate treatment.

Cardinal symptoms

Inattention

  • Difficulty in maintaining attention
  • Not paying attention to details, often making careless mistakes
  • Hard to complete tasks and instructions
  • Hard to organize tasks and activities
  • Avoid tasks that require continuous attention
  • Often lose items
  • Easily distracted by external stimuli
  • Forgetfulness in daily activity

Hyperactivity - impulsiveness

  • Restlessness or twisting of the hands and feet
  • Leaving seat when should be sitting
  • Running around in inappropriate situations
  • Hard to play or take part in activities quietly
  • Always "busy non-stop"
  • Excessively talkative
  • Answering questions before they are fully asked
  • Hard to wait for turns
  • Interrupting or disturbing others

Epidemiological data

Prevalence statistics

• Global prevalence rate: among children it is 5.9% to 7.1%, and among adults, it is approximately 2.5% to 4.4%
• Prevalence rate among Chinese children is approximately 6.26%, with an estimated 23 million child patients
• Gender difference: The prevalence rate among boys is approximately 2 to 3 times that of girls
• Genetic factors: The heritability is approximately 76%, making it a highly hereditary disease
• Comorbidity: Approximately 65% of ADHD patients are accompanied by other mental disorders

Treatment and Management

The treatment of ADHD adopts a multimodal approach, including medication, behavioral intervention, educational support and family training. For children over 6 years old, stimulant drugs (such as methylphenidate and amphetamine) are the first-line treatment options, with an effective rate of 70% to 80%. Non-stimulant drugs (such as tomoxetine and guanfaxin) are suitable for patients who cannot tolerate stimulants.
Behavioral therapy includes training in behavioral management skills, social skills training and cognitive behavioral therapy. The adjustment of the school environment, such as seat arrangement, task breakdown, and extra time, is also very important. Parent training helps to implement consistent behavior management strategies in the family environment.

Schizophrenia

Global prevalence rate: 0.3% - 0.7%

Brief introduction

Schizophrenia is a serious chronic mental disorder that affects an individual's thinking, perception, emotions and behaviors. The characteristic of this disease is impaired ability to verify reality, manifested as hallucinations, delusions, disordered thinking and abnormal behavior. Schizophrenia usually occurs in late adolescence or early adulthood, having a significant impact on patients' social functions and quality of life.
Although the exact cause of schizophrenia remains unclear, studies have shown that genetic factors, environmental factors and abnormal brain structure and function may all be involved. Early identification and treatment are crucial for improving the long-term prognosis of patients. Although it cannot be cured at present, many patients can lead a relatively normal life through comprehensive treatment.

Cardinal symptoms

Positive symptoms

  • Auditory hallucinations (most common)
  • Visual hallucinations or other sensory hallucinations
  • Persecutory delusion
  • Delusion of relation
  • Delusion of grandeur
  • Thoughts wander or break down
  • Lalopathy
  • Behavioral disorders or tension-related behaviors

Negative symptoms

  • Emotions dull or uncoordinated
  • Poverty of speech
  • Lack of willful activity
  • Social withdrawal
  • Anhedonia
  • Attention deficit
  • Declined ability of abstract thinking

Cognitive symptoms

  • Working memory defect
  • Executive dysfunction
  • Decreased attention and alertness
  • The processing speed slows down.
  • Language learning and memory problems

Epidemiological data

Prevalence statistics

• Global lifetime prevalence rate: approximately 0.3% to 0.7%, affecting about 24 million people worldwide
• Prevalence rate in China: approximately 0.6% to 0.8%, with an estimated 6 to 8 million patients
• Age of onset: usually between 18 and 25 years old for men and between 25 and 35 years old for women
• Gender difference: The prevalence rates of men and women are basically the same, but men develop the disease earlier
• Mortality rate: The average lifespan of patients is 15 to 20 years shorter than that of the general population
• Suicide risk: Approximately 10% of patients die by suicide

Treatment and Management

The treatment of schizophrenia centers on antipsychotic drugs, combined with psychosocial intervention. Second-generation antipsychotic drugs (such as olanzapine, risperidone, and aripiprazole) are usually the first choice because they have relatively fewer side effects. Drug treatment requires long-term maintenance. Even after symptoms are relieved, medication should still be continued to prevent recurrence.
Psychosocial intervention includes cognitive behavioral therapy, family education, social skills training, vocational rehabilitation, etc. Early intervention plans are particularly important for first-time patients and can significantly improve long-term prognosis. Community rehabilitation services help patients reintegrate into society, improve their quality of life and functional level.

Major Depressive Disorder

Major Depressive Disorder

Global prevalence rate: 4.4% - 5.0%

Brief introduction

Major depressive disorder is one of the most common mental illnesses, characterized by persistent sadness, a sense of despair and a loss of interest in daily activities. This disease not only affects the patient's mood, but also influences their thinking, behavior and physical health. Depression can affect people of any age and is one of the main causes of the global disease burden.
The pathogenesis of depression is complex, involving the interaction of genetic, biochemical, environmental and psychological factors. Neurotransmitter imbalance (especially serotonin, norepinephrine and dopamine) is regarded as a key factor. Stressful life events, chronic diseases, and certain drug and substance abuses may also trigger depression.

Cardinal symptoms

Core symptoms

  • Persistent sadness or a sense of emptiness
  • Losing interest or pleasure in the activity
  • Significant fatigue or lack of energy
  • Low self-worth or excessive guilt
  • Slow thinking or inability to concentrate

Physical symptoms

  • Sleep disorders (insomnia or excessive sleepiness)
  • Significant changes in appetite or weight
  • Psychomotor agitation or retardation
  • Headache, digestive problems
  • Chronic pain

Severe symptoms

  • Keep thinking of death
  • Suicidal thoughts or behaviors
  • Despair and a sense of helplessness
  • Social functions have been severely impaired
  • Psychotic characteristics (severe cases)

Epidemiological data

Prevalence statistics

• Global annual prevalence rate: approximately 4.4%, and the lifetime prevalence rate can reach 16.6%
• Prevalence rate in China: approximately 3.4% to 4.2%, with an estimated 54 million patients
• Gender difference: The prevalence rate among women is approximately twice that of men
• Age of onset: The first onset usually occurs between the ages of 20 and 30
• Recurrence rate: Approximately 50% to 85% of patients will experience multiple episodes
• Suicide risk: Approximately 15% of patients with major depressive disorder die by suicide

Treatment and Management

The treatment of depression usually includes medication, psychotherapy, or a combination of both. Antidepressant drugs include selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs), serotonin-norepinephrine reuptake inhibitors (SNRIs), etc. Drug treatment usually takes 4 to 6 weeks to show obvious effects, and the maintenance treatment period is recommended to be at least 6 to 12 months.
In terms of psychotherapy, cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT), interpersonal therapy (IPT), and psychodynamic therapy have all been proven effective. For severe depression, electroconvulsive therapy (ECT) and repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation (rTMS) are also effective treatment options. Lifestyle changes, such as regular exercise, a healthy diet and sufficient sleep, can also help improve symptoms.
